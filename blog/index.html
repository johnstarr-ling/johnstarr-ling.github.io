Interests:

Broadly, I am interested in computational psycholinguistics and its interfaces. More specifically, I am intrigued by the innate and incredible abilities that humans have regarding linguistic processing. Our minds so readily create, adapt, and probe the boundaries of our imaginations. Yet, it is evident that there is much work to be done before computational models can be considered (by any means) creative, understanding entities.

In short, I am asking the following questions: what makes humans so good at understanding really challenging constructions like irony, metaphor, and humor? How do computational models of language fail with these topics? When and where do they intersect, and why?

Projects:

  1. Mind Rhymes

    Mind rhymes are a previously-unstudied subset of the broader category of rhyme subversion, where the intended rhyming target is replaced by a new word or phrase. For an example, let's turn to the 1948 song "Shaving Cream" by Benny Bell:

    "I have a sad story to tell you./
    It may hurt your feelings a bit. /
    Last night I walked into my bathroom, /
    and stepped in a big pile of _____ ."

    What fills in the blank? Our knowledge of context, rhyme scheme, metrical structure, and other linguistic factors suggest that the blank should be filled by shit:

    "... and stepped in a big pile of shit!"

    However, the titular phrase is sung instead, subverting our expectations:

    "... and stepped in a big pile of shaving cream!"

    My work attempts to understand what licenses these mind rhymes using experimental and information-theoretic measures. Further, I am interested in the question of how humans and models recover the intended rhyming target upon hearing the subversion. This project is ongoing.

  2. Syllables and Nasals within the Languages of the DRC

    From a more theoretical linguistics perspective, I study the phonological patterning of nasals and syllables of the languages spoken in the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC). I aim to capture typological and areal variation within these languages through the use of Optimality-Theory (OT) constraints. This project is ongoing.

  3. Phonesthemes and Humor

    The relationship between a group of sounds (a word) and meaning is often assumed to be arbitrary. Exceptions to this concept of arbitrariness are constructions such as onomatopoeia, whose sound mimics their intended semantics: "woof!", "buzz...", "mooooo."

    Phonesthemes are yet another anomaly: they are groupings of sounds that have developed an underlying implied meaning. For example, the "-mp" at the ends of words like "bump", "stomp", and "dump" appears to imply some meaning of "forceful contact".

    I am interested in how these phonesthemes implicate humor, as found in Surreal Speak: stonk, succ, etc. and other odd examples. This project is an ongoing collobaration with my wonderful colleague Maksymilian Dąbkowski.
